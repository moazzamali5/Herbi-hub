How to Make a Herbarium


Actually making a herbarium is more easier then we think. With a few basic supplies, anyone may complete the enjoyable and easy hobby of making their own herbarium.


Before you collect the plant you must consider:

  1. Make sure you do not collect the plant at restricted area. For example, National Park, Forest or Private property.
  2. Do Not take more than one of the same species plant. You can collect the weeds whithout the limit but please reduce the disturb with the environment and avoid leaving the holes after take the plants.

Here got some ways to making an herbarium:



Steps One: Collection of Plant


Collection of plant is inportant because the plant of your collect will be the specimens for your future research. Here got some information about the plant collection


  • First of all, you must choosing the right plants which means the plants condition must be healthy, have completely developed leaves, come with 30-40cm in length included stems and roots is possible.
  • Avoid to collect diseased plants for herbarium.
  • Before you gather the plants, take thorough field notes about the location, habitat, date of collection and any relevant detail about the plant.
  • Then, use polythene bags or silver vasculum to store the collection and prees it while the plants are fresh. The purpose of doing this is to maintain moisture.


Steps Two: Pressing the Plant


The process of pressing plant is to preserve the morphological characteristics of the plant speciment while flattening and evenly dying it in order to mount and preserve it on a piece of hard paper. Here got some information and precaution about this process.

  • Think about how you want the dried specimens to appear when you are prepared to press your plant before you pressing the plant.
  • Make sure the leaves are visible from all sides, and rotate the blooms or fruits so that their fronts, sides, and backs are all visible.
  • Long plant specimen can be be trimmed in certain places or folded in a zigzag pattern and placed it inside the newspaper sheet.
  • For large portions specimen such as bark or large fruit specimen can be cut half and should be stored alongside with primary specimen in labelled.
  • Never let any part of the specimen hang from the press to avoid broken off of the specimen.
  • Tightening the straps with foot can make sure the press straps are securely tightened.
  • Be carfull to arange the plant pieces neatly and make sure all leaves and flowers are clearly visible before do pressing process.


Steps Three: Drying


When creating herbarium specimens, drying is an essential process since moisture can cause specimens to rot and lose their scientific value. Additionally, drying stops mould, discolouration, and irregular growth.

Here got some information about using drying to preserve of herbarium:

  • Plants in a herbarium are dried at temperatures close to 100¬∞C in a forced-air plant drier. In order to assist dry the plants more evenly, presses are positioned within the dryer so that hot air may be forced up through the cardboard corrugates.
  • Chanege the blotting paper
  • To prevent the grow of fungus due the drying process, replace the blotting paper on every 2-3 days or the blotting paper was moist.
  • Use a dehydrator or put the press next to a heat source to expeditiously dry the specimens.


Steps Four: Mounting the Specimen


Mounting the specimen can make your specimen look more nicer and it can preserve for a long time.

  • Once the specimen was fully dried, then Attach the specimen firmly using archival tape or glue, making sure that every piece is visible and evenly distributed.
  • After that, write the scientific name of the plant, the location of the collection, the date, the collector's name, and any additional characteristics on a thorough label on the right lower side of herbarium sheets.
  • If the plant has been adequately dried, it will be fragile, so if it is going to be on display or you will be handling it frequently, cover it with paper or plastic.
  • If the specimen is larger, itmust employ acid-free paper and specific glues and inks that do not decay in order to guarantee that their specimens will persist for hundreds of years.


Steps Five: Label the Specimen



Label is important for herbarium. It because it can identity the spesific information of the herbarium. It's Similar to how each object in a museum is uniquely identifiable by a number or barcode, curators of herbarium collections and scientists corresponding with one another globally require a clear means of referring to each particular specimen.


Label it on the right lower side of herbarium sheets and it must be included the information below:

  • Scientific name of the plant
  • Location of collection the collector's name, and any additional characteristics on a thorough label on the right lower side of herbarium sheets.
  • Date of collection
  • Name of person collect the plant
  • For addition, you may add the spesific information about the plant such as height, width or color.


Steps Six: Storage


The storage of herbarium is important because it can make herbarium in a prefect condition and easier to make retrieval and archive storage.

  • To avoid insects attack your specimens, kindly frezee herbarium specimens around 2 weeks to prevent the damage of the herbarium
  • Keep the herbarium in the dry and cool place.
  • Make sure herbarium cabinets are sealed to keep out light, humidity, and pests.
  • To regulate moisture, you can use silica gel packs or other desiccants.
  • Check for symptoms of moulds or silverfish on a regular basis.
